Presidents of Poland – A chronological list of Polish presidents and other individuals who held the office of the head of state of Poland since 1918.

Second Polish Republic (1918–1939)
From the May Coup of 1926 to his death in May 1935, Józef Piłsudski effectively held the highest power in the state, serving as the General Inspector of the Armed Forces, Minister of Military Affairs, and briefly as the President of the Council of Ministers.

Government of the Republic of Poland in Exile (1939–1990)
On September 30, 1939, President Ignacy Mościcki resigned from office, previously appointing Władysław Raczkiewicz as his successor (in accordance with the provisions of the April Constitution of 1935), making him the first president in exile. This office was a continuation of the presidency of the Second Republic. Until July 5, 1945, the Polish government-in-exile had full international recognition (except for the USSR, Nazi Germany, and its allies). On that same day, the Provisional Government of National Unity gained worldwide support. Most countries immediately accepted this situation and signed international agreements with the new Polish government. Formally, the government in exile could not continue its activities due to the lack of recognition by the international community; however, it functioned until the first general presidential elections in Poland in 1990, refusing to acknowledge many of the provisions of the Yalta Conference and the post-WWII status quo. For several (to dozens of) years, it was recognized by only a few countries (Ireland, Spain, Colombia, Cuba, Lebanon, Vatican, Venezuela, Australia).

Polish People’s Republic (1952–1989)
Chairman of the State Council of the People’s Republic of Poland (1952–1989)
The 1952 Constitution of the Polish People’s Republic abolished the office of president. Its role was taken over by the State Council (an office already existing under the Small Constitution of 1947) – a collegial constitutional head of state, with the Chairman of the State Council at its head. However, the most important figure in the People’s Republic of Poland was, in fact, the First Secretary of the Central Committee of the Polish United Workers’ Party. The following were the First Secretaries: Bolesław Bierut (1948–1956), Edward Ochab (1956), Władysław Gomułka (1956–1970), Edward Gierek (1970–1980), Stanisław Kania (1980–1981), Wojciech Jaruzelski (1981–1989), and Mieczysław Rakowski (1989–1990).

President of the Polish People’s Republic (1989)
In 1989, the State Council was replaced by the office of the President of the People’s Republic of Poland, which remained in effect until the election of the first and only President of the People’s Republic of Poland on July 19, 1989. On December 31, 1989, the constitutional law came into effect, changing the name of the country to the Republic of Poland.

Third Polish Republic (since 1989)
The first to hold the office was Wojciech Jaruzelski (previously President of the People’s Republic of Poland), starting December 31, 1989, when the constitutional law changing the country’s name to the Republic of Poland came into effect, until the swearing-in of Lech Wałęsa as the President of the Republic of Poland.

Length of Presidency
The longest-serving president of Poland was August Zaleski, who served as president in exile for 9,069 days. During the interwar period, the longest-serving president was Ignacy Mościcki (4,866 days), among the heads of state of the Polish People’s Republic it was Henryk Jabłoński (4,823 days), and in the Third Republic, it was Aleksander Kwaśniewski (3,653 days).

The person who temporarily held the office of president for the shortest period was Bogdan Borusewicz (1 day). The shortest-serving president of Poland was Gabriel Narutowicz, who held the office for 2 days. Among the presidents in exile, the shortest-serving was Ryszard Kaczorowski (521 days), among the heads of state of the Polish People’s Republic it was Józef Cyrankiewicz (461 days), and in the Third Republic, it was Wojciech Jaruzelski (356 days).

Age of the Presidents
The youngest president of Poland was Aleksander Kwaśniewski, who began his term at the age of 41. During the interwar period, the youngest was Stanisław Wojciechowski (53), among the presidents in exile, it was Władysław Raczkiewicz (54), and among the heads of state of the Polish People’s Republic, it was Bolesław Bierut (51).

The oldest president of Poland was Edward Raczyński, president in exile, who ended his term at the age of 95. During the interwar period, the oldest was Ignacy Mościcki (71), among the heads of state of the Polish People’s Republic, it was Henryk Jabłoński (75), and in the Third Republic, it was Wojciech Jaruzelski (67).

The youngest president of Poland on the last day of their term was Aleksander Kwaśniewski, who ended his term at the age of 51. During the interwar period, the youngest was Gabriel Narutowicz (57), among the presidents in exile, it was Ryszard Kaczorowski (71), and among the heads of state of the Polish People’s Republic, it was Bolesław Bierut (60).

The oldest president of Poland on the first day of their term was Edward Raczyński, president in exile, who began his term at the age of 87. During the interwar period, the oldest was Ignacy Mościcki (58), among the heads of state of the Polish People’s Republic, it was Wojciech Jaruzelski (62), and in the Third Republic, it was Bronisław Komorowski (58).

Ignacy Mościcki (resignation during his second term due to internment after leaving the occupied country)

Government of the Republic of Poland in Exile
Ryszard Kaczorowski (resignation after the swearing-in of the democratically elected president Lech Wałęsa, with the transfer of presidential insignia)

Term Shortenings:
None of the individuals holding the office of head of state after 1918 had their terms shortened by a ruling from the State Tribunal or being declared unfit for office. However, there were four cases of term shortening due to the abolition or replacement of the office or the method of its appointment:

Government of the Republic of Poland in Exile
Władysław Raczkiewicz (actual shortening of term in the country, resulting from the legal fiction of declaring the office vacant, though the presidency continued in exile)

People’s Republic of Poland
Bolesław Bierut

Third Republic of Poland
Wojciech Jaruzelski

Term Extensions:
Government of the Republic of Poland in Exile
August Zaleski (extension without legal grounds of the presidency, formally concluded in 1954, for an indefinite period, leading to a political split in exile and the creation of the Council of Three – an opposition center claiming the powers of the head of state)
